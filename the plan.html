06 · Viral Strategy

Tripping the Algorithm — What Actually Works

The Four Viral Post Formulas for Your Story

"I wrote a love story about two people at the end of the world. One line broke me while I was writing it. Here it is: [YOUR LINE]. It drops May 26. $2.99. Link in bio."

Why it works: Emotional specificity + low price point = impulse purchase. The single line creates desire to read the context around it.

"We're Naked! is NOT for you if you need love stories to end happily. It IS for you if you've ever loved someone so much that existing near them was its own kind of apocalypse."

Why it works: Exclusion triggers self-identification. People immediately want to prove they can handle it. They tag friends who "need this."

"I wrote this in Nanaimo, BC. Alone. In winter. It's the most honest thing I've ever made and I'm terrified you'll read it. Releasing May 26. I think I need you to read it."

Why it works: Vulnerability + geographic specificity = shareability. Local pride + emotional honesty is a combination that travels far.

"My story exists in three forms: a $2.99 digital download. A $100 HTML ordinal on the BSV blockchain — one of 50, ever. And an IRL physical edition you can ONLY get by burning your ordinal. Yes. Burning it. Some things are more beautiful when they can't be replicated."

Why it works: Genuinely novel. Tech Twitter, BSV collectors, NFT communities, and literary circles will all engage with this from entirely different angles — triple audience, one post.

Comment Bait That's Genuine

End posts with a question people actually want to answer:

  • "What's the last thing that felt like a kind of rapture to you?" — open and atmospheric, matches your story's world
  • "Tag someone who would read a love story that doesn't have a happy ending." — drives reach through tagging
  • "Finish the sentence: The worst kind of love is when ____." — high comment volume, deeply shareable
  • "Do you prefer love stories that end happily or tragically?" — poll format, then: "If you said tragic — I wrote something for you."

Hooks: First 3 Words Rule

On every platform, the first three words determine whether anyone reads further. Never start a caption with "I", "So", or "Hi." Instead:

  • "Something is ending—"
  • "You won't survive—"
  • "Nobody told me—"
  • "What if love—"
  • "The world ends—"

Live Strategy — What to Do Once You're On

  • Ask "Where are you watching from?" in the first 60 seconds. Comment engagement in the first minute signals the algorithm to push your Live to more people.
  • Read your opening paragraph. Then pause. Silence on a Live makes people stay to see what happens next.
  • Pin your purchase link in the Live comments before you start. Have a second window open so you can monitor comments while you're reading.
  • Do Q&A about the story's themes — not plot. Themes create emotional connection. "What does apocalypse mean to you?" after they've read it is the best possible follow-up conversation.
  • Save every Live. Clip 30–60 second highlights immediately. These become evergreen Reels and TikToks.

TikTok-Specific: Duet + Stitch Strategy

  • Find BookTok creators talking about atmospheric, surreal, or queer literary fiction. Duet or Stitch their content with your perspective. This puts you in front of their audience for free.
  • Use trending audio from dreamcore, dark academia, or literary niches. You don't have to chase trends — but trending audio gives each video a placement boost.
  • Post natively. Never just repost your Instagram Reel to TikTok as-is. Film a version for TikTok specifically, even if it's the same subject matter.

No other literary fiction author is minting ordinals and offering a physical burn redemption. That novelty gives you press angles in three completely separate media ecosystems: literary, tech/crypto, and arts culture. One story, three entirely different press pitches, zero audience overlap.

Lead with this in all your BSV communications. The resonance between the story's themes and the ordinal mechanic is what makes this genuinely interesting to literary and tech audiences alike.
